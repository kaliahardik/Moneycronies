Uncertainty to Resiliency: Canada's Path for 2026

Hardik Kalia January 26, 2026 4 min read

I attended a "Year Ahead" economic panel at the Rotman School of Management expecting a backward-looking exercise in damage control. Instead, it felt like a checkpoint.

Last year, the defining word was uncertainty. This year, it seemed to be resiliency.

That shift matters. But as an investor, I left convinced of something more nuanced: resilience showed up, but it showed up unevenly, and it is already being misinterpreted as validation. Resilience is a condition. It is not a plan.

1. Canada absorbed the shock. It did not neutralize it.

The Canadian economy avoided a recession in 2025. Growth came in below trend, but positive. From a distance, that looks like success. From a capital allocation lens, it looks more conditional.

What struck me most was not the aggregate outcome, but how narrowly resilience was distributed. Trade-related stress did not wash across the country evenly. It pooled. Southwestern Ontario is living a different economic cycle than large parts of British Columbia or Quebec. That matters because policy is national, but risk is local.

For investors, this means two things. First, averages are becoming less informative. Second, dispersion is rising across regions, sectors, and households. Dispersion creates opportunity, but it also breaks blunt policy tools.

2. Monetary policy has largely done what it can. That is the risk.

One of the most important insights from the panel was implicit rather than explicit: monetary policy is no longer the dominant variable.

Rates helped cushion demand and stabilize expectations. But much of today's pressure is supply-driven or structural. Higher rates do not fix labour shortages driven by demographics. They do not accelerate permitting timelines. They do not resolve internal trade barriers. They do not meaningfully improve productivity.

From an investor's perspective, this matters because returns now depend more on execution than easing. If growth relies on rate cuts to reaccelerate, the upside is capped. If growth relies on structural reform and capital redeployment, the upside is larger but slower and far less certain.

In short, the macro baton is being passed from the central bank to governments. Markets should be pricing that transition more explicitly.

3. The consumer story is not weak. It is bifurcated.

The panel pushed back, correctly, on the idea that affordability concerns are merely "vibes." The mistake is not optimism or pessimism. The mistake is aggregation.

A meaningful share of Canadian households entered this period with balance sheet strength, asset exposure, and no mortgage. Another meaningful share did not. For the latter group, inflation in essentials has outpaced wage gains in lived terms, regardless of what averages suggest.

Why does this matter for investors? Because consumption risk is asymmetric. The marginal consumer drives marginal demand. Canada is already seeing a housing adjustment through softer prices, weaker turnover, and stretched affordability. As mortgage renewals roll through 2026 at materially higher rates, discretionary spend will compress mechanically, even without a forced or disorderly housing correction. That is not a crisis forecast. It is arithmetic.

4. Trade risk is now behavioral, not mechanical.

Much of the discussion around CUSMA missed what I think is the key point. The biggest economic cost is not tariffs themselves. It is decision paralysis.

Businesses delay hiring. Capex is deferred. Strategic investments are put "on pause" because the rulebook feels provisional. Even if worst-case outcomes never materialize, uncertainty raises hurdle rates. For capital allocators, this increases the premium on visibility and balance sheet resilience. It also explains why investment has lagged even as headline growth held up. The economy functioned. Confidence did not fully recover.

5. Canada's long-term optionality is real, but patience is wearing thin.

Perhaps the most striking comment was that global investors appear more constructive on Canada's long-term position than Canadians themselves.

That resonates. The world is repricing security, supply chains, and access to scarce resources. Canada screens well on all three. Critical minerals, energy, water, food, and institutional stability are not cyclical advantages. They are structural ones.

But markets do not pay for potential. They pay for execution.

Canada's productivity problem is well understood. The debate is no longer diagnostic. It is operational. Internal barriers, regulatory timelines, and capital formation constraints are now the swing factors. These are solvable problems. They are also politically difficult ones, which is why patience, not consensus, is the binding constraint.

6. The investment takeaway.

If I distill the evening into an investment view, it is this: Canada has proven more resilient than feared, but resilience alone will not compress risk premiums. The next leg of returns depends on whether breathing room turns into capacity.

If Canada executes on productivity, internal trade, and capital deployment, the gap between global optimism and domestic skepticism closes, and long-term assets reprice upward. If it does not, resilience becomes a ceiling rather than a foundation.

Surviving the shock was necessary. It was not sufficient. 2026 will tell us whether Canada understands the difference.
