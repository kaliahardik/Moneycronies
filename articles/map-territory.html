Confusing the Map With the Territory Has Always Been Expensive

Hardik Kalia March 10, 2026 10 min read

Charlie Munger often repeated a deceptively simple mental model borrowed from the Polish philosopher and engineer Alfred Korzybski:

The map is not the territory.

A map can describe the world, but it is not the world itself. It is only a simplified representation of something far more complex. Imagine someone asking where the best coffee shop nearby might be. A map application can show a list of locations with ratings and directions. But that map cannot tell you whether the cafe is unexpectedly closed today, whether the line is currently twenty minutes long, or whether the shop is packed with people who have already decided it is worth visiting. The map shows information about the world. The territory reveals what people are actually doing inside it.

Investors often forget this distinction when evaluating technology businesses. It is easy to focus on the visible interface that users interact with every day while overlooking the deeper infrastructure that actually generates the economic advantage. That mistake appears frequently in the current debate surrounding artificial intelligence and the future of Google search.

A familiar narrative has spread across technology media, investor discussions, and conference panels. Artificial intelligence assistants such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity are widely described as the systems that will finally break Google's dominance over how people find information online. According to this narrative, conversational AI will replace traditional search engines, and the most dominant advertising business ever built will gradually lose relevance as users migrate toward systems that answer questions directly.

But there is a more basic economic question underneath this discussion that almost nobody seems interested in asking. Google Search supports roughly $180 billion dollars in annual advertising revenue across an enormous ecosystem of businesses that depend on search traffic. If search truly becomes less relevant, where do those marketing dollars actually go? Advertising budgets do not disappear because a new interface becomes fashionable. Instead, they follow user intent.

That is the real issue. Google's moat is not the search box. It is the behavioural data layer underneath it, built over more than 25 years of observing what people actually do. Until AI assistants gain access to a comparable stream of real world behavioural signals, the claim that Google's dominance is collapsing is much less straightforward than the popular narrative suggests.

The Number Everyone Is Watching

Much of the discussion about Google's supposed decline begins with a single statistic that appears regularly in headlines about the future of search. Google's global search market share peaked at approximately 92.9% in 2023 before slipping slightly below 90% the following year. By late 2024 the figure had fallen to roughly 89% globally, while the United States drifted toward the high eighties.

That decline has generated extensive commentary about the death of search engines and the rise of conversational AI as the new gateway to the internet. AI assistants now process enormous volumes of prompts each day, and many users increasingly ask AI systems questions before opening a traditional search engine. Apple executives even testified in court that Safari search activity experienced measurable declines for the first time in more than two decades.

These changes are real and deserve attention. Ignoring the competitive pressure created by AI assistants would be as careless as assuming Google is untouchable. But market share numbers alone do not explain the economics of search. Not every query represents the same kind of problem.

Two Different Types of Questions

There are broadly two categories of questions people type into search engines, and they require different kinds of infrastructure to answer well. The first category consists of informational questions where the answer exists in documents already available somewhere on the internet. These include questions about how compound interest works, who invented the telephone, why inflation occurs, or how a specific historical event unfolded.

AI assistants are extremely well suited to these queries. Their architecture is designed to synthesize written information into coherent explanations. Instead of presenting a list of links, they generate a direct response by compressing information across sources. For this class of query, the disruption to Google is real and likely to intensify as models improve.

Google is not absent from this shift. The company's own large language model, Gemini, operates inside the same search ecosystem that already handles billions of queries each day. Unlike standalone assistants, Gemini sits directly on top of Google's search infrastructure and therefore inherits the same indexing systems and real-time data streams produced by users across Google's products.

At the same time, Google has not clearly led the AI model race so far. Systems such as GPT-4 and Claude have often ranked ahead of Gemini in quality benchmarks and early user adoption. That gap matters in the short term because the most capable model tends to attract experimentation and developer attention. But model leadership can change quickly, while infrastructure advantages built over decades are harder to replicate.

The second category is different. These are queries about decisions in the physical world rather than information stored in documents. People search for restaurants that are open nearby, plumbers with strong recent reviews, pharmacies carrying a specific medication, or hotels available in a neighborhood tonight. These answers depend on real time conditions, physical location, and the accumulated behaviour of thousands of people making similar decisions.

The distinction between these two categories turns out to matter far more than most commentary acknowledges.

A Midnight Craving That Made Something Click

A small experience made this distinction clear to me. Late one evening near Bloor and St. George in Toronto, I was walking home after a long day and looking for something quick to eat. I briefly considered asking an AI assistant for restaurant recommendations. Then I realized that the question I actually needed answered was much simpler. I needed to know which restaurant nearby was open right then, within walking distance, and still serving food.

That was not primarily an information problem. It was a real-world decision problem. I opened Google Maps instead and found a place two blocks away with strong ratings, a kitchen open until midnight, and recent reviews from people who had clearly visited late in the evening. The decision required almost no effort because the system already reflected a pattern of real behaviour behind the search. Understanding why that works requires looking beneath the search interface itself.

What Google Actually Built

Most people think of Google as a search engine that ranks websites and sells advertisements next to the results page. Investors often describe it as a platform that monetizes user intent. Both descriptions are broadly true, but neither captures what makes Google difficult to displace.

The first layer is the crawler, which continuously scans the web to discover new pages and revisit existing ones. Google operates one of the largest automated discovery systems ever built, sending bots across the internet to map enormous portions of the public web.

The second layer is the index, which stores and organizes what the crawler finds into a massive searchable database. When a user types a query, Google retrieves results from this index rather than scanning the internet in real time.

The third layer is ranking and retrieval, which determines what appears in response to a specific query. This is the layer where AI assistants appear most disruptive, because language models can generate answers instead of presenting links. But ranking sits on top of crawling and indexing. Replacing the interface is one challenge. Recreating the underlying infrastructure is another entirely.

The Behavioural Data Layer

Even that description is incomplete, because Google's advantage is not just about organizing the internet. It is also about observing what happens after information is presented.

Through Google Maps, Android devices, Chrome, and billions of daily interactions with its services, Google collects behavioural data about how people move through the physical world. The company can observe which businesses people search for and then visit, how long they stay, whether they return, and which routes they take.

This dataset is fundamentally different from the textual information used to train language models. AI assistants learn primarily from documents describing the world. Google, by contrast, has built systems that absorb signals generated by the world itself.

That difference matters enormously for queries tied to real-world decisions such as where to eat, where to stay, or which service provider to choose. It also explains why Google is integrating artificial intelligence directly into its own ecosystem. Gemini operates inside Google's products rather than as a separate system disconnected from them. Even if competing models temporarily outperform Gemini on benchmarks, they still operate without access to the same underlying behavioural signals.

Models can improve in months. Behavioural datasets of global human activity accumulate over decades.

However, this advantage is not immune to disruption. Regulators have begun challenging the mechanisms that helped Google accumulate that data at scale. In 2024 a U.S. federal court ruled that Google had illegally maintained aspects of its search monopoly, partly through distribution agreements such as its default placement on Apple's Safari browser. If future remedies weaken those arrangements, the flow of behavioural signals feeding Google's ecosystem could slow over time. The moat may still exist, but the rate at which it deepens could change.

AI Is Building Its Own Maps

The infrastructure landscape has also evolved quickly. Early versions of AI assistants relied heavily on existing search engines to access live information. That dependency is now weakening.

OpenAI has built its own crawler, GPTBot, which collects content directly from the web. Perplexity operates its own crawling and indexing infrastructure as well. Several AI companies have signed direct licensing agreements with publishers to secure access to content.

In other words, AI systems are beginning to build their own maps of the internet. But crawling and indexing primarily describe documents. They explain what information exists online and where it can be found. They do not automatically capture how people behave after encountering that information.

The Feedback Loop Nobody Is Pricing

Another dynamic is developing beneath the AI vs. Google debate. In 2024 Google introduced AI Overviews, which generate summarized answers at the top of search results pages before users click external links. From Google's perspective the strategy is understandable. It keeps users inside the ecosystem and reduces the incentive to switch to standalone AI assistants.

For publishers and content creators, however, the consequences have been significant. Before AI Overviews, the top ranked organic result often captured roughly 15% of clicks. After the feature launched, some studies suggest that figure dropped materially in many cases. Certain publishers have reported traffic declines approaching 90% for particular categories of queries.

If those patterns persist, a feedback loop could emerge. Publishers lose traffic, publishers lose revenue, and over time fewer organizations invest in producing content. The web itself becomes thinner. Systems designed to summarize the internet may gradually have less original material to summarize.

What This Means for Google

Google clearly faces genuine pressure from the rise of AI assistants. Informational queries are increasingly handled by systems that synthesize answers directly rather than routing users through lists of links. Younger users in particular often prefer conversational interfaces when the task involves research or explanation. In the early phase of the AI boom, companies such as OpenAI and Anthropic have often produced models that users perceive as more capable than Google's Gemini.

But technological transitions rarely unfold as cleanly as the public narrative suggests. The key question is not whether AI will change how people access information. It almost certainly will. The more interesting question is how the infrastructure behind high-intent commercial queries evolves over time.

If AI systems succeed primarily at summarizing documents, the competitive dynamics may look very different from a world in which they also replicate the behavioural signals generated by billions of real-world decisions.

For investors trying to understand Google's future, that distinction may be the most important territory to watch.
