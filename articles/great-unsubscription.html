The Great Unsubscription

Hardik Kalia February 23, 2026 15 min read

Warren Buffett advises that when a man finds himself in a leaky boat, energy devoted to changing vessels is usually more productive than energy spent patching the leak. For much of the last decade, investors believed they had finally found a vessel in technology that did not leak.

SaaS was being priced as a toll road that seemed immune to erosion, because the toll was modest, traffic flowed in one direction, and the surrounding terrain offered no viable detours for those who depended on it. Each new customer could simply use what was already built, just like another vehicle entering a road without requiring new construction. To investors, these businesses appeared dependable because the software sat directly in the path of daily work, embedded in operations, and customers kept paying as long as they stayed on the route.

Analysts grew comfortable modeling these businesses as though they were regulated infrastructure assets disguised as software companies, because revenue appeared predictable, gross margins expanded with scale, and renewal became less of a negotiation and more of an administrative routine. The narrative was not merely optimistic, but sounded mathematical. Retention rates exceeded 90%, net revenue retention climbed above 100%, and the incremental cost of serving additional users approached zero, creating the impression that growth and stability had somehow fused into a single financial instrument.

The investment thesis of the SaaS story rested on three pillars. Code was scarce, and because it was scarce, it functioned as a moat. Complex enterprise systems required large engineering teams, deep domain expertise, and years of iteration, which created the reasonable belief that replication was expensive, slow, and unlikely to occur at scale without enormous capital commitment. Retention was structural: once embedded into payroll systems, customer databases, compliance workflows, or financial reporting processes, the product ceased to be discretionary and became part of the operating skeleton of the firm, and because removal carried operational risk, renewal felt almost automatic. And revenue growth depended on seats: revenue expanded in proportion to headcount, and because businesses historically added employees over time as they grew, the software vendor's income statement appeared structurally tethered to economic expansion.

The underlying conviction was that customers would continue to pay not because the software was irreplaceable in principle, but because replacing it would be inconvenient in practice. Friction became the hidden moat, and its steady financial outcomes discouraged anyone from asking about its durability.

Recently, two structural shifts have occurred simultaneously. Software has become easier to replicate because of AI, challenging the first pillar. And the human labor that justified a large number of software seats has begun to contract due to automation, challenging the third.

The toll road has not disappeared. The cars have learned to drive themselves while the city has begun building side streets. The Great Unsubscription begins not with customers leaving en masse, but with the quiet erosion of the premises that once made leaving unnecessary.

The Mechanics of the Unsubscription

Structural change in business rarely announces itself through visible collapse. It typically reveals itself first through shifts in economic geometry rather than immediate revenue discontinuities. To understand what is occurring in software markets today, two variables are more determinative than quarterly growth rates or headline retention metrics.

The first concerns the marginal cost of replication. If a product can now be rebuilt or approximated rapidly and inexpensively through modern AI tooling, then the historical moat of code loses a meaningful portion of its defensive power. The second concerns the human labor base that justified seat-based monetization. If automation reduces the number of employees required to perform the workflows the software supports, then revenue becomes structurally detached from organizational scale.

When these two variables are placed on orthogonal axes, four distinct economic positions emerge, each carrying implications that differ materially from the assumptions embedded in legacy SaaS valuation models.

In the first quadrant, where products are easy to build and seats are shrinking, economic erosion occurs without visible churn. Contracts renew, logos remain intact, and retention metrics appear stable, yet the labor base underwriting seat-based revenue contracts steadily under automation. The economic density per account declines while reported customer count remains unchanged. Revenue compresses quietly, and the deterioration is not immediately classified as churn because the relationship itself persists.

In the second quadrant, where products are easy to build but seats remain stable, the threat emerges through pricing rather than volume. Replication abundance accelerates feature parity and erodes differentiation, narrowing the space for premium positioning. Customers gain negotiating leverage, incumbents defend share through bundling and discounting, and margin structure begins to reflect competitive abundance rather than engineered scarcity.

In the third quadrant, where replication remains difficult and seat stability persists, economic durability is highest. These firms control deeply embedded integration layers, regulatory embedding, and proprietary data gravity that protect them from rapid displacement. Even here, however, durability is not static, because automation's influence on labor intensity operates gradually and compounds over time.

In the fourth quadrant, where products are difficult to replicate but seats are shrinking, durability persists at the system-of-record level while monetization logic deteriorates. These companies sit at integration choke points or control proprietary datasets that cannot be displaced easily, yet automation reduces the number of human operators interacting with their systems. Survival remains plausible, though growth assumptions must give way to consumption or outcome-based logic.

Before AI automation pressure became visible, capital within the SaaS sector was weighted heavily toward business models that were difficult to replicate and supported stable seat expansion. As AI reduces replication cost and compresses seat intensity, that capital weighting shifts. The redistribution is not about immediate revenue collapse but about revised expectations for how long excess returns can persist.

A SaaS customer can retain in exactly the same way a car might still pass through a toll booth. The contract does not churn, but the flow of work gradually diverts off the platform. What was once a $200,000 core workflow license quietly shrinks into a $30,000 maintenance fee.

Retention measures whether the road still exists. It does not tell you whether the traffic still depends on it.

The Real Fight Is Not Products. It Is Profit Pools.

SaaS is not collapsing. It is being reorganized around where economic control resides within the enterprise stack. If agents perform the workflow, then the interface becomes secondary and the economic surplus migrates toward the layer that controls authorization, data, compliance, and distribution. The profit pool no longer belongs to the tool that facilitates the work, but to the system that governs and records it.

In the pre-AI era, value accrued primarily at the workflow layer, because labor executed inside the application and seat expansion tracked organizational growth. In the emerging structure, value concentrates in systems that own identity, permissions, audit trails, regulatory evidence, and integration webs, because these elements anchor operational continuity when automation begins performing the underlying tasks.

This is not about user experience or aesthetic loyalty. Enterprise switching costs are functions of governance risk, integration fragility, reporting integrity, and compliance exposure. Software that embeds itself within control planes behaves like infrastructure, while software that merely facilitates tasks behaves like a feature.

The paradox is that many AI products are still priced per seat, per month, despite marketing narratives that imply labor replacement. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and numerous enterprise copilots bill against headcount authorization structures that were inherited from SaaS, which means the monetization unit remains human even when the execution layer is increasingly automated.

Automation alters what the software does. Vendors will still charge on a recurring basis, but the basis of that charge may gradually move from human seats toward usage, throughput, or embedded platform access. Firms that successfully realign billing with how work is actually executed will preserve recurring revenue durability. Firms that cling rigidly to headcount as the billing anchor will experience pressure as labor intensity declines.

Who Survives

The survivors will not be defined by branding themselves as AI companies or SaaS companies. They will be defined by their ability to operate in two economic systems simultaneously. Call them Bridge Companies.

They can function inside enterprise governance structures that demand compliance, integration stability, auditability, and procurement discipline, while simultaneously deploying agentic workflows that reduce labor intensity and align pricing with measurable output. Most firms lack this dual capability. They are either operationally trusted but technologically rigid, or technologically agile but institutionally fragile.

Survivors exhibit four structural traits. They own or control a system of record or sit at an integration choke point that other workflows depend upon. They possess proprietary datasets that materially improve model performance rather than merely wrapping commoditized intelligence in interface layers. They can meter economic value through throughput, transactions, or outcomes instead of counting seats tied to human presence. And they already possess embedded enterprise distribution, which reduces the friction associated with introducing automated execution into production environments.

These characteristics do not guarantee growth. They increase the probability that excess returns persist long enough to justify durable valuation.

Conclusion

Every technology cycle eventually becomes a sorting mechanism that distinguishes structural advantage from temporary positioning.

The internet did not eliminate retailers. It exposed those who confused physical presence with defensible economics. Cloud computing did not eliminate software vendors. It exposed those who confused license ownership with genuine customer dependency. Artificial intelligence will not eliminate SaaS. It will expose those who mistake recurring billing inertia for enduring economic strength.

What is unfolding is a reclassification across the software industry. Early growth lifts many participants upward along the adoption curve, but only a smaller group transitions into structural compounding as conditions mature and competitive pressures intensify.

The Great Unsubscription is not a collapse in demand for software. It is the curve resetting expectations across the SaaS landscape as capital reassesses durability under new technological physics.

Capital will not reward yesterday's expansion narrative. It will reward businesses whose economics continue to compound even after the easy slope of the curve begins to flatten. When conditions shift, greatness is not inherited from prior cycles. It is re-earned by those positioned to climb the next curve rather than slide down the last one.
