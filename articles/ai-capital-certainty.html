On AI, Capital, and the Limits of Certainty

Hardik Kalia February 4, 2026 10 min read

Lately, I have found myself increasingly uneasy listening to conversations about artificial intelligence. Not because the technology is unimpressive. On that point, there seems to be broad agreement. What troubles me is something else: the confidence with which enormous amounts of capital are being committed under the assumption that technological importance will naturally translate into economic success.

History suggests that this is a familiar mistake. We are watching some of the largest companies in the world commit unprecedented sums to AI-related infrastructure. Over the past twelve months, AI-related technology spending across the S&P 500 has exceeded $1.25 trillion, with approximately 28% of that total concentrated among the largest technology platforms. The scale alone makes clear that these commitments are no longer incremental or theoretical. They take the form of power contracts, data-center construction, and equipment orders that lock in capital years before the economics of utilization and monetization are fully understood. At the same time, the language used to justify this spending often relies on inevitability rather than evidence.

That combination, heavy and irreversible capital commitment paired with limited visibility into returns, is usually where risk quietly accumulates.

The Limits of Familiar Intuition

Much of the confidence surrounding current AI investment appears to rest on a familiar intuition. For decades, software rewarded capital generously. Once developed, it scaled at low marginal cost, with revenues growing far faster than the capital required to support them. That experience trained investors to expect rapid operating leverage and early economic validation when new technologies gained adoption.

A related comparison is often made to the early internet era. That analogy is understandable, but incomplete. The internet required substantial upfront investment in fiber, data centers, and networking infrastructure. Yet much of the value ultimately accrued at the application layer, where capital requirements were modest and scalability was extraordinary. As bandwidth became abundant and costs declined, new services could grow rapidly without demanding proportional increases in physical infrastructure.

AI at scale appears different. Intelligence itself consumes scarce resources. Computation, power, cooling, and physical space are not merely enabling inputs. They are ongoing economic costs. As usage expands, consumption expands with it. Unlike the internet, where marginal users were cheap, AI users impose real and recurring demands on infrastructure.

As capital budgets and infrastructure plans become clearer, it is increasingly evident that AI behaves less like software and more like infrastructure. Capacity must be built well in advance of demand. Capital must be committed before utilization is proven. Returns, if they materialize, tend to arrive later and less smoothly than familiar technology intuition would suggest.

Infrastructure investments can be highly profitable over time. They can also be unforgiving when capital is deployed ahead of economic readiness. The risks are not primarily technical. They center on timing, utilization, and discipline. These risks tend to be least visible precisely when enthusiasm is highest and extrapolation feels safest.

Power Is the First Irreversible Commitment

At scale, AI systems consume extraordinary amounts of electricity and require dense, highly specialized facilities to operate reliably. In many cases, the energy consumed by inference now exceeds that required for initial model training. What begins as a computational challenge quickly becomes a physical one.

Securing power, cooling, grid access, and physical space cannot be done incrementally. These requirements demand early commitment through long-term power contracts, purpose-built data centers, and specialized infrastructure. Once made, those commitments are difficult to unwind. Capital is fixed long before utilization and monetization are fully understood.

This matters because power is often the first constraint encountered and the earliest source of irreversibility. Once capital is deployed to solve the power and capacity problem, it cannot be easily redeployed elsewhere. The investor's clock begins running well before clarity on returns emerges.

From that point forward, outcomes depend less on continued technological progress and more on how effectively the surrounding system adapts around sunk capital. The risk is not that the technology fails, but that the economics struggle to keep pace with the commitments already made.

Efficiency Reduces Costs, Not Commitment

Efficiency improvements are real, and they matter. Over time, chips become faster and more energy efficient, and the cost of computation declines. That dynamic has shaped technology economics for decades and continues to do so today.

What efficiency changes is what new systems can do. It does not unwind the commitments embedded in systems already built. Much of today's AI infrastructure has been designed around specific assumptions about power density, rack weight, cooling requirements, and electrical distribution. Those assumptions are baked into buildings, grids, and balance sheets.

Newer generations of hardware improve performance per watt, but they also concentrate more compute into denser and heavier configurations. In many cases, this places additional strain on floors, racks, cooling systems, and power delivery. Rather than enabling simple substitution, technological progress often requires further investment before earlier infrastructure has fully paid for itself.

History offers a familiar parallel. For decades, Moore's Law reduced the cost of individual transistors, yet total capital spending in semiconductors continued to rise. Efficiency at the component level did not simplify the system. It expanded it. Investment accumulated in layers, and returns depended less on efficiency itself than on whether demand grew fast enough to absorb an ever larger base of fixed costs.

AI infrastructure appears to be following a similar path. Efficiency lowers unit costs, but it does not reduce commitment. The real risk for investors is not that the technology fails, but that utilization and cash generation lag long enough for the next efficiency wave to force yet another round of capital that cannot be taken back.

When New Capacity Precedes Economic Payback

The infrastructure is built around assumptions about power density, rack weight, cooling methods, and network layout. Earlier generations of systems were designed around those assumptions. Newer generations increasingly strain them.

In practice, this means that technological progress does not automatically free previously committed capital. In many cases, it does the opposite. It forces incremental investment before earlier investments have earned an adequate return.

This dynamic is familiar from other capital intensive industries. The transition from 4G to 5G wireless networks did not render existing infrastructure obsolete. Towers, sites, and backhaul remained in place. What changed was the equipment mounted on them. For extended periods, operators ran 4G and 5G networks in parallel to maintain coverage, compatibility, and performance. Capital was duplicated rather than replaced, and returns were pressured during the overlap.

A similar pattern appears to be emerging in AI infrastructure. Earlier facilities are not wasted, but they can become economically second best well before they are obsolete. New capacity is layered on top of old. Utilization becomes uneven. Returns are delayed.

None of this requires AI to disappoint technologically. It requires only that progress arrives faster than infrastructure can adapt and faster than capital can be repaid.

When Being Early Carries Its Own Cost

At this stage, the dominant uncertainty becomes timing. If efficiency gains arrive faster than infrastructure can be upgraded, capital remains tied up in suboptimal configurations. If monetization lags while capacity expands, utilization suffers. When utilization suffers, returns compress.

This pattern is well known in capital cycles. Being early often looks indistinguishable from being wrong for extended periods. Investors can be directionally correct and still experience disappointing outcomes if capital is deployed ahead of economic readiness.

This is where confidence becomes dangerous. Certainty encourages scale before validation and speed before discipline. When capital is committed on the assumption that demand will arrive on schedule, patience is consumed long before returns appear.

The risk, then, is not that AI fails to progress. It is that progress arrives on a timetable that capital cannot afford.

Where Economic Reality Sets the Boundary

Over time, economics reassert discipline. When capacity is expensive and usage continuously consumes scarce resources, not every application can justify its existence. The dividing line is not technical sophistication, but economic verification.

Where AI replaces measurable costs, reduces labor, or improves clearly monetizable outcomes, value becomes visible. Where outcomes are ambiguous and supervision remains intensive, the technology may impress without paying for itself.

Scarcity has a way of clarifying priorities. As capital costs rise and utilization becomes more discriminating, resources flow toward applications that generate durable returns and away from those that rely on expectation rather than evidence.

Limits of My Conviction

It is possible that this view underestimates the system's ability to adapt. The largest technology platforms have strong balance sheets and operational flexibility. They can absorb periods of overlap and underutilization that would strain smaller participants. Optimization at the software and model level may also reduce the need for frontier infrastructure across many commercially viable use cases. Demand could arrive faster and more cleanly than prior capital cycles would suggest.

It is also possible that today's spending reflects disciplined positioning rather than excess. Early investment may create optionality that more than compensates for near-term inefficiencies, particularly if scale and integration prove decisive advantages.

I do not dismiss these possibilities lightly. They represent the primary reasons to remain cautious in drawing firm conclusions before the economic evidence is clearer.

What Will Inform My View

Rather than draw firm conclusions, it is more useful to focus on evidence. Over time, outcomes will reveal whether capital is being rewarded for its patience.

I am watching utilization rates of AI infrastructure relative to capacity additions. I am watching whether incremental revenue growth keeps pace with incremental capital deployed. I am watching how quickly older infrastructure is repurposed rather than sidelined. And I am watching whether the language around spending shifts from inevitability toward discipline.

These signals will not resolve uncertainty quickly. But taken together, they will indicate whether this cycle is moving toward economic validation or simply accumulating commitment.

A Closing Observation

Artificial intelligence will almost certainly reshape large parts of the economy. That outcome alone does not guarantee attractive returns for the capital committed to make it possible. History suggests that the greatest risks often arise not when investors doubt change, but when they assume its economic rewards are assured.

I do not claim to know how this cycle will unfold. I am increasingly convinced, however, that the greatest danger lies in certainty, particularly when it accompanies large and irreversible commitments of capital.

In environments like this, caution is not pessimism. It is recognition of the difference between technological progress and economic return.
